Hardware Design

Drone Tracking for Dual-Comb Laser Spectroscopy

In a joint project between the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), and the University of Colorado I was part of a small team of graduate students developing a system to track an Unmaned Aerial System (UAS) with high precision at long range. The point of doing so was to bounce a laser beam back to its source so that it could be used to determine the composition of gases in a remote area.

The team had to design a tracking system that was capable of high precision in real time and integrate that tracking system with a ground based 2-axis gimbal that would perform the pointing of the laser. We implemented real time coordinate transformations and wrote in between translation software to coordinate the different systems. Quick integration and testing was crucial for this project as our time was limited to the duration of the school year.

Optical Cavity Resonator

Design and testing of an optical cavity resonator. In this experiment a laser beam is reflected back upon itself. Changing the phase diference of the self intersecting beam allows for the creation of different laser modes. Through this experiment my team learned about stabilizing and optical system (see the metal bricks on the optical breadboard) and we learned how to pick different laser modes out from a 732nm green laser source. Other interesting results that came out of this research project included observations of lens distortion caused by the heating of the lens by the laser.

Electric Guitar Amplifier

Design and implementation of a 'wah' pedal amplifier. 'Wah' pedals are commonly used by guitarists to add flair and effects to their music. The circuit was comprised of transitor, inductor and capacitor elements that would create a bandpass filter. This bandpass filter had a tuneable resonant peak that could be changed by the guitar player creating the 'wah' effect.
